# Logo Archetypes

Every logo falls into roughly one of these archetypes. Pick deliberately — the archetype shapes every other decision.

## Wordmark (logotype)
Text only, styled distinctively. Examples: Google, Coca-Cola, FedEx, Visa.

**When to use:** The name is short-to-medium (2-12 characters), memorable, and the business wants the name itself to be the brand. Great for new companies that need to build name recognition. Avoids the "what is that icon even?" problem.

**Watch out for:** If the name is long or generic, the wordmark gets visually weak. Needs strong, distinctive type choices to carry it.

## Lettermark (monogram)
Initials or a single letter, styled distinctively. Examples: IBM, HBO, CNN, HP, Volkswagen.

**When to use:** The full name is long or unwieldy. Creates a compact, confident mark that works at small sizes.

**Watch out for:** If the letters have no distinctive treatment, they can look generic (every startup with a "G" uses a circle). Give the letters a twist — custom ligature, negative space, structural play.

## Combination mark (icon + wordmark)
A symbol paired with the company name. Examples: Slack, Adidas, Lacoste, Doordash.

**When to use:** Most general-purpose. The safest choice for most new businesses because the icon provides identity while the wordmark reinforces the name. Can later evolve into an icon-only mark once the brand is known.

**Watch out for:** The icon and wordmark need to feel like they belong together — same weight, consistent corner radii, compatible personality.

## Brandmark (pictorial/symbol)
Icon alone, no text. Examples: Apple, Nike, Twitter, Target.

**When to use:** Only for companies with enough recognition to go nameless, OR for startups where the name is almost impossible to pronounce/spell and the icon needs to do all the heavy lifting. Rarely the right call for a brand-new company.

**Watch out for:** Without a wordmark, the icon has to be extremely strong on its own. Hard mode.

## Emblem
Icon and text combined inside a unified container (badge, crest, seal). Examples: Starbucks, Harley-Davidson, NFL, BMW, most universities and sports teams.

**When to use:** When the brand wants to feel traditional, established, craft-y, or institutional. Great for coffee shops, breweries, heritage goods, sports teams, schools.

**Watch out for:** Emblems can be hard to scale down — lots of detail in a small footprint. Make sure the icon inside also works stripped out for small sizes (favicon, app icon).

## Abstract / geometric mark
An abstract shape that doesn't literally depict anything but has a distinctive form. Examples: Pepsi, Airbnb, Mitsubishi, Chase.

**When to use:** When nothing in the product/service lends itself to a literal icon, or when the business wants to avoid being pigeonholed by a literal symbol. Also great for holding companies or conglomerates.

**Watch out for:** Abstract shapes risk being meaningless. Anchor them with a rationale, even if it's simple ("two forms unified = partnership").

## Mascot
A character, person, or creature representing the brand. Examples: KFC, Mr. Peanut, Wendy's, Michelin.

**When to use:** For family/consumer brands that want warmth, personality, and storytelling. Excellent for food, entertainment, sports, kids' products.

**Watch out for:** Mascots are complex to render and often don't translate to tiny sizes. Usually paired with a simpler "icon" variant for favicons etc.
