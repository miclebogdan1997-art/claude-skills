# Color for Logos — Quick Reference

## What different colors signal

These aren't laws — they're the defaults that a Western audience reads into each color. Use them deliberately, and subvert them deliberately when you want to stand out.

- **Red** — energy, urgency, appetite, boldness. Food, entertainment, sports, sale-driven retail. Risky for finance/medical (alarm, bleed).
- **Orange** — friendly, playful, affordable, enthusiastic. Kids' brands, budget goods, food delivery.
- **Yellow** — optimism, warmth, cheap-and-cheerful (McDonald's, Post-it). Hard to use for "premium."
- **Green** — nature, growth, health, money. Finance, sustainability, wellness, food. Overused in these spaces, so consider a distinctive shade.
- **Blue** — trust, calm, professionalism, technology. The safest corporate choice, which is also why it's the most generic. If you pick blue, pick a distinctive blue.
- **Purple** — creativity, luxury, spirituality, imagination. Good for differentiation in crowded categories.
- **Pink** — modern feminine, playful, soft, sometimes radical (think Barbie, but also Lemonade insurance's defiant pink in a blue industry).
- **Black** — luxury, authority, fashion, timelessness. Pairs with anything. Often the most sophisticated choice.
- **White** — minimalism, purity, space. Rarely a primary but essential as a counterpoint.
- **Brown/tan** — craft, heritage, earthy, artisanal. Coffee, leather, outdoors.
- **Beige/cream** — warmth, natural, understated premium. Bougie DTC brands love cream.

## Palette construction rules of thumb

**One color + neutrals** is usually enough. A single distinctive hue plus black, white, and maybe one gray is a complete palette for 90% of brands. Resist the urge to add more.

**If you use two accent colors**, they should have tension — analogous colors (blue + teal) feel harmonious but bland; contrasting colors (blue + orange, purple + yellow) feel energetic and memorable.

**Pay attention to value (lightness) as much as hue.** A logo in two colors of the same lightness can look muddy. Make sure there's clear value contrast between your logo and common backgrounds.

**Accessibility.** Check contrast against white and black backgrounds. A logo that disappears on white because it's pale yellow is broken, not subtle.

## Recommended safe palettes

When you need a palette quickly and the user hasn't specified:

- **Modern tech**: `#0f172a` (slate-900) + `#3b82f6` (blue-500) + white
- **Warm craft**: `#1c1917` (stone-900) + `#d97706` (amber-600) + `#fef3c7` (amber-100)
- **Fresh health**: `#064e3b` (emerald-900) + `#10b981` (emerald-500) + `#ecfdf5` (emerald-50)
- **Premium editorial**: `#0a0a0a` + `#fafaf9` (stone-50) + one accent (`#b45309` amber-700, or `#7c2d12` orange-900)
- **Playful consumer**: `#fb7185` (rose-400) + `#1e293b` (slate-800) + white
- **Luxury understated**: `#1a1a1a` + `#c2a878` (warm tan) + off-white `#f5f1e8`

Pull hex codes from the Tailwind palette (tailwindcss.com/docs/colors) — it's a well-balanced, battle-tested set and saves you from eyeballing hex values.

## Tips on using color in the logo itself

- **Keep the monochrome version strong.** If the logo only reads when colored, the underlying form is too weak. Build in black first, then add color.
- **Two-tone logos work best** when one color dominates and the other is used as an accent (maybe 80/20, not 50/50).
- **Gradients should have a reason** — they should evoke motion, depth, or transition. If they're just there to look "modern," they're a liability (they don't print well and look dated fast).
